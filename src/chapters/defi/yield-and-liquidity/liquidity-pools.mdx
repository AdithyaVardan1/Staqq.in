# Liquidity Pools

A liquidity pool is a smart contract holding reserves of two (or more) tokens. These reserves power DEX trading. Anyone can provide liquidity — and earn a share of trading fees in return.

<InfoBox type="important">
  Educational content only. Providing liquidity involves impermanent loss and smart contract risk. Not financial advice.
</InfoBox>

## Why Liquidity Pools Exist

Before AMMs, DEXes used order books — traders had to wait for a counterparty. For most token pairs, this didn't work well because there wasn't enough trading activity.

Liquidity pools solved this: a pool of tokens always available to trade against, at a price determined by a formula. No counterparty needed.

## How They Work

1. A liquidity provider (LP) deposits an equal value of two tokens (e.g., 1 ETH + 2,000 USDC)
2. They receive **LP tokens** — a receipt representing their share of the pool
3. Traders swap against the pool, paying a fee (typically 0.05–1%)
4. Fees accumulate in the pool
5. When the LP withdraws, they get their share of tokens plus accumulated fees

## LP Tokens

LP tokens are ERC-20 tokens that represent your claim on the pool.

- If the pool has 100 ETH and 200,000 USDC, and you provided 10%, you have 10% of the LP tokens
- When you redeem your LP tokens, you receive 10% of whatever is in the pool at that time
- LP tokens can often be staked in other protocols to earn additional rewards

<InfoBox type="note">
  LP tokens are composable — they're just regular ERC-20 tokens. You can deposit them into yield aggregators, use them as collateral, etc. This is "DeFi Lego."
</InfoBox>

Fee earnings depend on:
- **Volume**: more trades = more fees
- **Pool size**: smaller pool share of a high-volume pool = more earnings per LP token
- **Fee tier**: higher fees per trade but fewer traders

## Risks of Providing Liquidity

**1. Impermanent Loss (IL)**
When asset prices change after you deposit, you end up with a different ratio of tokens than you started with. If one token pumps, you'd have been better off just holding. This is covered in the next chapter.

**2. Smart Contract Risk**
The pool contract could have a bug. Multiple AMMs have been exploited. Always use established, audited protocols.

**3. Fee Erosion**
In low-volume pools, fees may not compensate for impermanent loss.

## Key Takeaways

<InfoBox type="tip">
  - Liquidity pools are smart contracts holding token reserves that power DEX trading
  - LPs deposit token pairs and receive LP tokens as receipts
  - LPs earn a share of trading fees proportional to their pool share
  - Key risks: impermanent loss and smart contract vulnerabilities
  - LP tokens are composable ERC-20 tokens that can be used in other DeFi protocols
</InfoBox>
