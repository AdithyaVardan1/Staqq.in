# Layer 1 vs Layer 2

As crypto grew, base blockchains struggled to handle millions of transactions cheaply. Layer 2 networks were built to solve this — without sacrificing security.

<InfoBox type="important">
  This chapter is educational only. Not financial advice.
</InfoBox>

## The Scalability Trilemma

Vitalik Buterin described a fundamental challenge: a blockchain can only optimise two of these three properties at once:

- **Security** — resistant to attacks
- **Decentralisation** — many independent nodes
- **Scalability** — fast, cheap transactions

Bitcoin and Ethereum prioritise security and decentralisation, making them slow and expensive at scale.

## Layer 1 — The Base Blockchain

A Layer 1 (L1) is the foundational blockchain that handles its own security and consensus.

## Layer 2 — Built on Top

Layer 2 networks process transactions off the main chain and periodically settle on the L1. They inherit L1's security while dramatically increasing speed and reducing costs.

### Types of Layer 2

**Rollups (for Ethereum):**
- **Optimistic Rollups** — Assume transactions are valid; disputes resolved by fraud proofs (Arbitrum, Optimism)
- **ZK-Rollups** — Validity proved cryptographically before submitting to L1 (zkSync, StarkNet, Polygon zkEVM)

**Payment Channels:**
- **Lightning Network** — Bitcoin's L2; creates direct payment channels between parties for instant, near-zero fee BTC payments

**Sidechains:**
- **Polygon (PoS)** — Ethereum-compatible chain with its own validators; cheaper and faster but not a true rollup

## Real-World Example

Sending ETH on Ethereum mainnet during peak congestion: ~$30–$100 fee, 15–30 second confirmation.

The same transaction on Arbitrum (an L2): ~$0.10 fee, under 1 second confirmation — while ultimately secured by Ethereum's security.

<InfoBox type="note">
  Polygon is often called an L2 but is technically a sidechain with its own security. Arbitrum, Optimism, and zkSync are true rollups that inherit Ethereum's security.
</InfoBox>

## Why This Matters

- **DeFi and NFTs** mostly live on Ethereum L1 and its L2s
- **L2 tokens** (ARB, OP) represent governance and sometimes fee-sharing in these networks
- **Scalability improvements** drive broader adoption — cheap transactions = more users
- **Bitcoin's Lightning Network** enables micro-payments (e.g., pay-per-article, streaming sats)

## Key Takeaways

<InfoBox type="tip">
  - The scalability trilemma: you can't easily have security, decentralisation, AND scalability at once
  - Layer 1 = base blockchain (Bitcoin, Ethereum) — secure but slower/expensive
  - Layer 2 = built on L1 — fast and cheap, inherits L1 security
  - Ethereum L2s include Arbitrum, Optimism (Optimistic Rollups) and zkSync, StarkNet (ZK-Rollups)
  - Bitcoin's Lightning Network is its main Layer 2 for cheap, fast BTC payments
</InfoBox>
