# DAOs & Governance

A DAO (Decentralised Autonomous Organisation) is an organisation run by code and community vote rather than a board of directors or management team.

<InfoBox type="important">
  Educational content only. Not financial or investment advice.
</InfoBox>

## What is a DAO?

Traditional organisations have a CEO, board, and management hierarchy that make decisions. A DAO replaces this with:

- **Smart contracts** that enforce rules automatically
- **Governance tokens** that give holders voting rights
- **On-chain voting** where proposals are implemented by smart contract, not executives

The internet's first widely-discussed DAO was "The DAO" on Ethereum in 2016 — which raised $150M and was subsequently hacked for $60M due to a smart contract bug, causing a major controversy.

Modern DAOs are far more sophisticated and power some of the largest DeFi protocols.

## How DAO Governance Works

1. A **proposal** is submitted (e.g., "Change the trading fee from 0.3% to 0.25%")
2. Token holders **vote** for or against — usually weighted by tokens held
3. If the proposal passes (typically >50% or a supermajority), the smart contract **executes it automatically**
4. No CEO signature required

<InfoBox type="note">
  Governance power is proportional to token holdings. This means large token holders (whales, VCs) have outsized influence — a significant criticism of many DAOs.
</InfoBox>

## Types of DAOs

- **Protocol DAOs** — govern DeFi protocols (MakerDAO, Uniswap)
- **Investment DAOs** — pool capital to invest (Metacartel, The LAO)
- **Service DAOs** — provide services like design/dev to other DAOs (RaidGuild)
- **Social DAOs** — community and membership-based (Friends With Benefits)
- **Collector DAOs** — pool funds to buy NFTs or assets (PleasrDAO bought the original "Doge" photo)

## Challenges and Criticisms

**Voter apathy:** Most token holders don't vote. Governance is often dominated by a small active minority.

**Plutocracy:** Those who hold the most tokens have the most power — similar to a traditional shareholder structure.

**Speed:** Governance votes take days to weeks, making fast responses to crises difficult.

**Legal ambiguity:** Most countries don't legally recognise DAOs. Members may face personal liability.

<InfoBox type="warning">
  Governance tokens can have speculative value based on the protocol's success, but they are not equity. Holding a governance token does not entitle you to protocol revenue unless the governance specifically votes to enable it.
</InfoBox>

## Key Takeaways

<InfoBox type="tip">
  - A DAO is an organisation governed by token holders via smart contracts, not a management hierarchy
  - Governance tokens give voting rights proportional to holdings
  - Major DeFi protocols (Uniswap, Aave, MakerDAO) are governed by DAOs
  - Challenges include voter apathy, whale dominance, and legal ambiguity
  - Governance tokens ≠ equity — they don't automatically entitle you to revenue
</InfoBox>
