# Whale Watching

In crypto, "whales" are large holders — wallets or entities controlling enough coins to meaningfully move markets. Tracking their activity can provide insights into upcoming price moves.

<InfoBox type="important">
  Educational content only. Whale watching is an imprecise art with many false signals. Not financial advice.
</InfoBox>

A common rule of thumb: any wallet holding >1,000 BTC is considered a whale.

## Why Whale Activity Matters

Because blockchains are public, you can see exactly what every wallet does. When a whale moves funds:

- **Exchange inflow** (whale sends to exchange) → potential selling pressure
- **Exchange outflow** (whale withdraws from exchange) → likely accumulation (moving to cold storage)
- **Between wallets** → may be internal movement, or transfer to OTC desks

## Bitcoin Whale Concentration

Glassnode tracks "whale addresses" (those holding >1,000 BTC or >10,000 BTC). Periods when whale count rises (accumulation) historically precede bull runs. When whales distribute (count falls), it can precede corrections.

<InfoBox type="note">
  Exchanges hold massive amounts of BTC for their users — their wallets don't represent the exchange's own speculation. Nansen and Arkham label known exchange wallets to help filter out this "noise."
</InfoBox>

## Limitations of Whale Watching

**Internal transfers:** A whale moving funds between their own wallets looks like a sale but isn't. Most Whale Alert transactions are internal.

**OTC trades:** Large blocks of crypto are often traded OTC (over-the-counter) directly between parties — never touching an exchange. These don't appear in order books.

**Spoofing:** Whales know they're being watched. They can make transactions that create false signals to trigger retail buying/selling before doing the opposite.

**Delayed reaction:** By the time Whale Alert notifies you, the market may have already priced in the move.

<InfoBox type="warning">
  Whale watching signals should be used as one input in a broader analysis, not as a standalone trading signal. Many retail traders have lost money acting on whale alert notifications without context.
</InfoBox>

## Key Takeaways

<InfoBox type="tip">
  - Whales are large holders; their on-chain moves are publicly visible on the blockchain
  - Exchange inflows (deposits) may signal upcoming selling; outflows suggest accumulation
  - Tools: Whale Alert, Nansen, Arkham, Glassnode for tracking
  - Most Whale Alert transactions are internal moves — not indicative of buying or selling
  - Whale watching is noisy and subject to manipulation; use as one input, not a standalone signal
</InfoBox>
