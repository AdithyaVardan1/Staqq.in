# Crypto Order Books

On centralised exchanges, an order book records every buy and sell order waiting to be filled. Reading it tells you where supply and demand are concentrated.

<InfoBox type="important">
  Educational content only. Order book analysis has limitations and should not be used as the sole basis for trading decisions. Not financial advice.
</InfoBox>

## How an Order Book Works

The order book has two sides:

**Bids (buy orders)** — buyers waiting to purchase at or below a certain price
**Asks (sell orders)** — sellers waiting to sell at or above a certain price

**Best bid** — highest price a buyer will pay right now
**Best ask** — lowest price a seller will accept right now

**Spread** — the gap between best bid and best ask

## Example Order Book

```
ASKS (Sellers)
₹52,000   |  0.50 BTC
₹51,800   |  1.20 BTC
₹51,500   |  2.00 BTC
──────────────────────
₹51,200   |  1.80 BTC   ← Best Ask
₹51,100   |  3.50 BTC   ← Best Bid
──────────────────────
₹51,000   |  2.20 BTC
₹50,800   |  0.90 BTC
BIDS (Buyers)
```

Spread = ₹51,200 - ₹51,100 = ₹100

If you place a **market buy order** for 1.8 BTC, you'll get filled at ₹51,200 (best ask).

## Market Depth

Market depth (or "depth of market") shows cumulative bid and ask volume at each price level. A depth chart visualises this.

**Deep market:** Large volume on both sides — hard to move price with one trade
**Shallow market:** Thin volume — a large order can spike price significantly

For Indian traders: BTC and ETH pairs on CoinDCX/WazirX have reasonable depth. Smaller altcoins have very thin books.

## Reading the Order Book for Signals

**Large buy wall (bid wall):** A cluster of buy orders at a particular price — can act as support. May also be spoofed.

**Large sell wall (ask wall):** A cluster of sell orders — acts as resistance. Also can be spoofed.

**Spoofing:** Placing a large order with no intention of filling it to influence price — then cancelling before execution. This is illegal in traditional markets but difficult to enforce in crypto.

<InfoBox type="warning">
  Order book analysis has significant limitations in crypto. Large orders are frequently spoofed (placed and cancelled to manipulate perception). On-chain markets (DEXes) don't have traditional order books — AMM pools serve a similar function but work very differently.
</InfoBox>

## Slippage

When your order is large relative to the available liquidity at the best price, you'll pay more than the quoted price. This is **slippage**.

Example: You want to buy ₹50 lakh of BTC. The best ask has only ₹10 lakh available. Your order consumes that and the next several price levels.

Calculate expected slippage using the order book depth before placing large orders.

## Key Takeaways

<InfoBox type="tip">
  - Order books show all pending buy (bids) and sell (asks) orders with quantities
  - Spread = best ask - best bid; tighter spread = more liquid market
  - Market depth shows available liquidity at each price level
  - Large bid/ask walls can indicate support/resistance but may be spoofed
  - Large orders cause slippage — check depth before placing significant trades
</InfoBox>
