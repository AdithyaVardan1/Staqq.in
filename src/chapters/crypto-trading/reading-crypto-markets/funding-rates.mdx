# Funding Rates & Open Interest

Funding rates are unique to crypto's perpetual futures market. They're one of the most useful sentiment indicators available to traders.

<InfoBox type="important">
  Educational content only. Futures trading involves significant risk including total loss of margin. Not financial advice.
</InfoBox>

## What are Perpetual Futures?

Unlike traditional futures contracts that expire on a set date, **perpetual futures** (perps) have no expiry. You can hold a long or short position indefinitely.

They're the most traded instrument in crypto — daily perp volume often exceeds spot volume by 5–10x.

## The Funding Mechanism

Perps stay close to the spot price through a **funding rate** — a periodic payment between long and short position holders.

**Positive funding rate:**
- Longs pay shorts
- Happens when the perp price is trading above spot (more demand for longs)
- The market is bullish

**Negative funding rate:**
- Shorts pay longs
- Happens when the perp price trades below spot (more demand for shorts)
- The market is bearish or fearful

Funding is paid every 8 hours on most exchanges. The rate might be 0.01% (tiny) or 0.1%+ (significant if sustained).

<InfoBox type="note">
  Extreme funding rates are often a contrarian indicator. When everyone is long (high positive funding), there are few new buyers left to push price higher — making a reversal more likely. Conversely, extreme negative funding means most traders are short, setting up for a violent short squeeze.
</InfoBox>

## Open Interest

**Open Interest (OI)** is the total notional value of all outstanding futures positions.

- **Rising OI + rising price** → New money entering longs — bullish confirmation
- **Rising OI + falling price** → New money entering shorts — bearish confirmation
- **Falling OI + rising price** → Short covering (shorts exiting) — momentum may be weaker
- **Falling OI + falling price** → Long liquidations — capitulation

## Liquidation Cascades

When price moves sharply against a large number of leveraged positions, those positions get liquidated automatically. This forces the exchange to close them at market — further accelerating the price move.

This is why crypto drops can be so violent: a 5% move might liquidate billions in positions, pushing price another 10%, liquidating more positions, etc.

<InfoBox type="warning">
  High aggregate open interest + high positive funding = large pool of leveraged longs waiting to be liquidated if price falls. This is often called an "overleveraged market" and precedes sharp corrections.
</InfoBox>

## Key Takeaways

<InfoBox type="tip">
  - Perpetual futures have no expiry and use funding rates to stay pegged to spot price
  - Positive funding = longs pay shorts (bullish market); negative = shorts pay longs (bearish)
  - Extreme positive funding = crowded longs, potential reversal risk
  - Open interest measures total outstanding futures positions — rising OI confirms trends
  - Liquidation cascades happen when high OI unwinds rapidly — causes violent moves
</InfoBox>
